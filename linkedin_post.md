# LinkedIn Post

while on spring break, i built an investment research thesis on Credo.

- 59 companies analyzed: Claude, GPT, and Gemini models stress-testing my assumptions in parallel

- an interactive web app as the final deliverable:

https://www.perplexity.ai/computer/tasks/credo-technology-interactive-p-2dAt6NAOTQOLlKklNq0VLg?view=thread#1



https://github.com/ivrjnvirutb/Perplexity-stock-pitch-competition-April-2026



Perplexity's stock pitch competition is judged by 3 of the most respected names in institutional investing:

Philippe Laffont (Coatue Management), Daniel Loeb (Third Point LLC), and Kenneth Hao (Silver Lake).



i'm not a financial analyst.

i don't have a bloomberg terminal.

i don't have a team.

yet, here's what i've realized:



AI is a complete paradigm shift in the same category as electricity and the internet.



in the age of AI, "half-assing" something looks a lot like what a senior professional could produce pre-AI.

that's not an insult to professionals, it's a statement about how fast the floor just rose.



and like every paradigm shift before it, the people who adapt first won't be the ones who had the most resources.

they'll be the ones who understand what it really means for execution to be nearly free.



for individuals, the gap between having an idea and acting on it has collapsed. a student on spring break can now compete with institutional research teams. not because AI thinks for you, but because it removes the friction between your thinking and its output.



for businesses, competitive advantage is no longer locked in headcount or capital. it's locked in judgment: the ability to frame the right problem and recognize a good answer when you see one.



but AI tools are only effective if you bring something to them first.



i didn't open Perplexity and ask it to write me a stock pitch. i first:

- spent time understanding the platform's capabilities before designing a single task

- profiled the judges (their frameworks, what they find lazy, what earns their respect)

- built a thesis grounded in civilizational history before touching a single company

- defined what the final output needed to look like before i wrote the first prompt



that's the distinction that matters, and it's the one most people are still getting wrong:

- using AI to skip the thinking is how you produce average outputs at scale 

- using AI to extend your thinking is how you produce something that couldn't have existed before



the bottleneck in this new world isn't access to tools, it's the quality of human judgment behind them.



soft skills aren't soft anymore, they're the hardest thing left to replicate.



intuition. taste. the ability to feel when something is off before you can articulate why. the willingness to sit with a problem long enough to develop a real point of view before handing it to a machine.



AI doesn't flatten human potential, it amplifies it for anyone willing to keep thinking.



and that's what makes this moment more hopeful than it is frightening.
